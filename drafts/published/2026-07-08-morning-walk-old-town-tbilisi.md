<p>Before eight in the morning, Old Tbilisi still belongs to the people who live here. Shopkeepers roll up shutters, a street cat claims a warm step outside a bakery, and the Kura River carries a thin layer of mist that burns off by nine. If you're staying with us, this is the walk we send guests on before breakfast &mdash; a loop of maybe twenty minutes that covers more of the old city's character than most day tours manage in three hours.</p>

<h2>Start at Metekhi Bridge</h2>
<p>Step out from Maison Metekhi and you're already at the river. Cross Metekhi Bridge slowly &mdash; it's narrow enough that you'll want to stop twice: once for the view upstream toward Narikala fortress and the cable car line climbing the ridge, and once for the view down, where the old bathhouses of Abanotubani send up thin curls of steam even in summer. The Metekhi Church sits just above the bridge on its cliff, rebuilt many times over since the 5th century, and it's usually empty enough at this hour that you can hear the river underneath the singing if a service is on.</p>

<h2>Into the Betlemi quarter</h2>
<p>From the church, follow the cobbled lane that climbs into Betlemi. This is the part of Old Town that photographs get made from &mdash; leaning wooden balconies, carved doors painted in shades that have faded to something better than the original color, courtyards with grapevines trained over washing lines. Don't worry about a fixed route here; the streets are short and forgiving, and the worst outcome of getting turned around is finding a set of stone stairs you wouldn't have otherwise. Locals are used to early walkers and won't mind if you glance into an open courtyard, though we'd say look rather than wander in.</p>

<h2>Down to Meidan Square</h2>
<p>Betlemi spills you out, eventually, onto Meidan Square, the old caravan crossroads where Armenian, Persian, and Georgian merchants once traded side by side. At this hour the square is mostly locals: a man setting up a folding table of secondhand books, someone sweeping in front of a carpet shop before it opens. It's worth pausing at the small mosque and the Jvaris Mama church nearby, both reminders of how many different communities have called this square home over the centuries. The souvenir stalls that crowd the square by midday haven't opened yet, which makes it easier to actually see the architecture instead of the awnings.</p>

<h2>Where to finish</h2>
<p>If you still have time before breakfast, walk one block further to the bakery on the square's north corner &mdash; ask for shoti fresh from the tone oven, and eat it on the walk back rather than waiting until you're home; it doesn't keep its warmth for long. Otherwise, retrace your steps along the river, where by now the mist has usually cleared and Metekhi Church catches the sun properly for the first time that day.</p>

<p>We time our own breakfast service to catch guests coming back from exactly this kind of walk &mdash; hungry, a little more oriented in the city than they were an hour before, and usually already asking where lunch should be. It's a good problem to have in Tbilisi.</p>
