<p>Georgian wine doesn't taste like wine made anywhere else, and the reason is underground, literally. Qvevri &mdash; the large clay vessels used to ferment and age wine on the skins for months at a time &mdash; give white grapes a copper-amber color and a grip of tannin you won't find in a bottle from anywhere in Western Europe. You don't have to travel to Kakheti to taste it properly. Some of the best qvevri wine in the country is poured within fifteen minutes of Maison Metekhi's front door.</p>

<h2>Chardin Street and its wine bars</h2>
<p>Cross the river and climb toward Sioni, and you'll hit Chardin Street, a narrow lane that's become the unofficial spine of Tbilisi's natural wine scene. Vino Underground is the one to know first: a cellar-level bar run by a cooperative of small producers, with a list that changes constantly and staff who will talk you through the difference between a Rkatsiteli aged six months on the skins and one aged two years. Ask for something from Kakheti if you want the classic amber style, or a Racha red if you want something lighter and more food-friendly.</p>

<h2>g.Vino and the wine-bar cluster near Sioni</h2>
<p>A few doors down, g.Vino keeps a shorter, more curated list and a better selection of snacks to go with it &mdash; order the nadugi with herbs and a plate of jonjoli if they have it, and let the bartender pick a wine to match. This stretch of Old Town rewards wandering between two or three places in one evening rather than committing to just one; portions are small, glasses are generous, and nobody minds if you order a taste before a full pour.</p>

<h2>What to actually order</h2>
<p>If you only try one style, make it a qvevri-aged white from Kakheti &mdash; Rkatsiteli or Mtsvane are the grapes to look for, and the color should be genuinely orange, not a marketing gimmick. For reds, Saperavi is the grape that shows up on almost every list, dense and dark with a natural acidity that stands up to Georgian food better than most imports. Don't be shy about asking whether a wine is qvevri-made or produced in steel tanks in the European style; most bars in this part of town are proud to tell you, and the difference in the glass is obvious once someone points it out.</p>

<h2>When to go</h2>
<p>Early evening, before the after-dinner crowd arrives, is the easiest time to get a seat and an unhurried conversation with whoever's pouring. Chardin Street fills up fast after nine, especially on weekends, and while nobody will turn you away, you'll get more attention and better recommendations if you arrive around six or seven. It's a five-to-ten minute walk from the hotel across Metekhi Bridge and up through Betlemi, which makes it easy to treat as a pre-dinner stop rather than a whole evening's plan &mdash; though most people end up staying longer than they intended.</p>
