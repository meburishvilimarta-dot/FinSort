<p>The three terms get used almost interchangeably in vendor decks, and that causes real confusion when a central bank or a commercial bank is trying to scope a project. They describe three different buyers solving three different problems, and the distinction matters as soon as budget and requirements enter the conversation.</p>

<h2>FinTech serves the end customer, not the regulator</h2>
<p>FinTech is technology built by or for financial services companies to serve their own customers &mdash; payments apps, digital lending, robo-advisors. It's the layer most people encounter directly. FinTech companies are typically the ones being regulated, not the ones building tools for regulators, which is where the next two categories start to diverge from it.</p>

<h2>RegTech helps regulated institutions comply</h2>
<p>RegTech (regulatory technology) is software that helps a regulated entity &mdash; a bank, an insurer, a payments provider &mdash; meet its own compliance obligations more efficiently: automated transaction monitoring, know-your-customer checks, regulatory report generation, and tools like an offline validator that lets an institution check, sign, and submit a report securely without depending on a live connection to the regulator's portal. The buyer is the regulated firm, and the goal is reducing the cost and error rate of staying compliant.</p>

<h2>SupTech is RegTech's mirror image, built for the supervisor</h2>
<p>SupTech (supervisory technology) flips the buyer: it's technology built for the regulator or central bank itself, to collect, validate, and analyze the data that regulated institutions submit. Where RegTech automates a bank's side of reporting, SupTech automates the supervisor's side &mdash; ingesting submissions across multiple channels, validating them against rulesets before they're accepted, flagging anomalies with risk-scoring models, and giving supervisors a consolidated view across every institution they oversee instead of a folder of spreadsheets from each one. A central bank running a modern integrated regulatory platform is doing SupTech; a commercial bank submitting through it is doing RegTech.</p>

<h2>Why the distinction changes the procurement conversation</h2>
<p>Getting this right up front avoids the most common stalling point in these projects: a regulator's IT team scoping a "RegTech" RFP when what they actually need is a supervisory platform with identity and access management, audit logging, and multi-institution data synchronization built for the regulator's side of the relationship. The practical differences that should show up in requirements include:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Who logs in.</strong> SupTech platforms need role-based access for supervisors across departments and, often, secure messaging channels out to the institutions they oversee. RegTech tools are usually scoped to a single institution's compliance team.</li>
  <li><strong>What "validation" means.</strong> For a supervisor, validation happens on intake, against rules that may change institution by institution or sector by sector. For an institution, validation happens before submission, checking its own report against a known schema.</li>
  <li><strong>Where the AI gets applied.</strong> Supervisors increasingly want risk scoring and anomaly detection across an entire portfolio of submitting institutions; individual institutions want AI closer to document processing and internal query handling, including chatbots that can answer staff or customer questions without pulling in a compliance officer for every routine case.</li>
</ul>

<h2>One platform, two audiences</h2>
<p>In practice, the two sides need to talk to each other, which is why the more capable platforms in this space are built to serve both: a central hub where institutions submit and validate data, and supervisors receive, cross-check, and analyze it, with the legislative and policy context both sides need kept current in one place rather than scattered across circulars and PDFs. That's the model FINA's Integrated Regulatory Platform is built around, alongside standalone tools like an offline submission validator for institutions that need to prepare and sign reports before connectivity is available.</p>

<p>None of this makes FinTech, RegTech, and SupTech competitors to each other &mdash; they sit at different points in the same reporting relationship. Knowing which one you're actually buying is what keeps a procurement process from stalling six weeks in over a requirements document that was scoped for the wrong side of the table.</p>
