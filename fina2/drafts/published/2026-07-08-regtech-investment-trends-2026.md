<p>RegTech - regulatory technology used by financial institutions to meet their own compliance obligations more efficiently - has moved well past the pilot stage. The question worth asking now isn't whether banks and regulators are adopting it; survey data already puts that close to universal. It's where the next wave of spending is actually going, and whether the infrastructure underneath it can keep up.</p>

<h2>Adoption is mainstream, and the spending keeps climbing</h2>
<p>Ninety-five percent of financial institutions now report scaled use of RegTech in at least one regulatory domain, and that's the baseline this year's spending decisions are being made against, not the frontier. Roughly 63% of firms surveyed plan to increase their RegTech spending in 2026, with expected spend from surveyed institutions alone running into the billions. That tracks with the compliance cost pressure driving it: Bain & Company estimates that 15-20% of banks' operational expenses already go to governance, risk, and compliance functions, which is exactly the cost base RegTech is meant to bring down. When a fifth of operating cost sits in one function, even a modest efficiency gain from better tooling is large enough in absolute terms to justify a serious budget line, which is part of why RegTech spending keeps growing even in years when overall IT budgets are flat.</p>

<h2>The money is shifting toward automation, not more dashboards</h2>
<p>Where that spending goes next is changing shape. RegTech vendors raised more than $5 billion in disclosed funding in 2025, and AI agents and agentic automation are expected to become one of the largest single areas of investment within risk and compliance over the next few years - a shift from tools that surface information for a human to review toward tools that carry out defined parts of a compliance workflow themselves. That's a different investment thesis than the dashboard-and-reporting tools that defined the category a decade ago, and it changes what "RegTech spending" is actually buying.</p>

<h2>What regulators themselves are building looks different by design</h2>
<p>On the supervisory side, the pattern isn't one platform fitting every institution - it's targeted infrastructure matched to a specific gap. The National Bank of Georgia has prioritized building an integrated data platform that consolidates regulatory reports, market data, consumer complaints, and macroeconomic indicators into one view. The Qatar Financial Centre Regulatory Authority is deliberately growing its financial-analysis-and-innovation staffing - currently just under 10% of divisional headcount, expected to rise toward 15% - specifically to blend supervisory domain expertise with data science and machine learning skills, rather than treating that as a separate specialist function. Peru's banking and insurance superintendency has invested in link-analysis tools for anti-money-laundering work that visualize relationships across large transaction datasets to surface patterns a manual review would miss. None of these are the same investment, because none of these institutions had the same starting gap - which is itself a useful corrective to any pitch that treats SupTech as a single off-the-shelf category rather than infrastructure that has to match a specific supervisory mandate.</p>

<h2>The thing every adopter has in common is also the thing still unresolved</h2>
<p>A 2024 survey of thirteen central banks and two supervisory authorities across five regions found that every single respondent using RegTech or SupTech relied on data standards during data collection - without exception. What the same survey also found is that those standards vary significantly even among institutions operating in similar regions and facing similar reporting requirements. That's the gap the investment numbers don't show: adoption has outpaced standardization, which means a growing share of institutions now run capable individual systems that still can't easily exchange data with each other, or with the institutions they supervise.</p>

<p>The RegTech story in 2026 isn't really about whether the technology gets used - that part is settled. It's about whether the standards underneath all this new investment get built with the same urgency as the tools sitting on top of them.</p>
