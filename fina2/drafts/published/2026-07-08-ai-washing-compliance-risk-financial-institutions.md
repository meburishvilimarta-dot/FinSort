<p>"AI-washing" is the practice of describing a product or process as AI-driven when the underlying technology is far more limited than the marketing suggests - a rules engine rebranded as "intelligent," a chatbot with a scripted decision tree called "conversational AI." In most industries that's a reputational risk. In financial services, where product claims are regulated speech, it's becoming an enforcement risk, and 2026 is the year that distinction started showing up in actual cases rather than just warnings.</p>

<h2>Regulators are treating AI claims as ordinary disclosure claims</h2>
<p>The core legal point is not new, and that's exactly what makes it dangerous: a false or exaggerated claim about an AI system's capabilities is evaluated the same way as any other false claim about a financial product, under the same anti-fraud and disclosure rules that already existed before "AI" was in the sentence. Regulators including the SEC have signaled that aggressive enforcement against AI-washing doesn't require new AI-specific law - existing rules against misleading investors or customers already cover a firm that says its system "uses AI" to detect fraud or optimize a portfolio when it substantially doesn't. That's a meaningfully lower bar to enforcement than waiting for AI-specific regulation to catch up.</p>

<h2>The pressure to overstate AI capability is structural, not accidental</h2>
<p>Competitive pressure is a big part of why this keeps happening: firms that aren't visibly "doing AI" read as behind in a market where AI adoption has become a proxy for technological credibility. That pressure is compounded by how fast adoption is genuinely moving - industry surveys now put a large majority of banking firms using some form of agentic AI, with only a minority having it fully deployed and the rest still running pilots. When "using AI" covers everything from a production system making real decisions to an early pilot that touches one workflow, the gap between what a marketing page implies and what a compliance officer could actually defend under questioning gets wide fast.</p>

<h2>The gap is usually in governance, not the model itself</h2>
<p>Most AI-washing findings don't turn on whether a model works - they turn on whether the institution can show how it works, who reviewed its outputs, and what happens when it's wrong. A firm that deployed a genuinely capable model but never documented its testing, its failure modes, or its human review process is in nearly the same enforcement position as one that never had a real model at all, because the claim being tested is "we can show this does what we said," not "does the technology exist." This is why governance frameworks - documented testing, defined human-in-the-loop checkpoints, a clear audit trail from output back to the data and logic that produced it - matter as much to defensibility as the AI itself.</p>

<h2>What a defensible AI claim actually requires</h2>
<p>The practical response looks less like a legal disclaimer and more like ordinary product documentation, extended to cover the model:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Scope the claim to what's deployed, not what's planned.</strong> A pilot running on 5% of transactions is not the same claim as a system that's "AI-powered," full stop.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep a record of what the model was tested against and how it performed</strong>, not just that it was tested.</li>
  <li><strong>Document the human review step</strong> for any AI-influenced decision that affects a customer or a regulatory submission, and be able to show it happened, not just that it's policy.</li>
  <li><strong>Update the claim when the system changes.</strong> Marketing copy written when a pilot launched often survives, unedited, long after the system it described has changed.</li>
</ul>

<p>None of this requires a different attitude toward AI adoption - it requires the same discipline financial institutions already apply to any other regulated claim, applied consistently to the newest one. The firms that get caught out by AI-washing enforcement rarely lacked the technology; they lacked the paper trail to back up what they said about it.</p>
